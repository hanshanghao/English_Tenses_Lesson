What is Ramadan?

Ramadan is the ninth month of the Islamic (Hijri) lunar calendar. In 2026, it begins on 18 February and ends on 18 March, lasting 30 days. It is the month during which the Holy Quran was first revealed to Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and it is observed worldwide by over 1.8 billion Muslims through fasting, prayer, reflection, and charity.

شَهْرُ رَمَضَانَ الَّذِي أُنزِلَ فِيهِ الْقُرْآنُ هُدًى لِّلنَّاسِ
"The month of Ramadan is the one in which the Quran was revealed as guidance for mankind..."
— Surah Al-Baqarah 2:185

The 5 Pillars & Fasting (Sawm)

Fasting in Ramadan is the fourth of the Five Pillars of Islam — the core practices every Muslim must observe. During Ramadan, Muslims abstain from food, drink, smoking, and sinful behaviour from Fajr (dawn) until Maghrib (sunset) every day for the entire month.

The fast is not merely about hunger — it is an act of worship, self-discipline, empathy for the poor, and drawing closer to Allah. Every healthy adult Muslim is required to fast, with exceptions for the sick, elderly, pregnant women, travelers, and children.

2026 Dates in Taiwan

Ramadan 2026 in Taiwan: 18 February – 18 March 2026. Suhoor (pre-dawn meal) in Taipei ends around 5:30–6:00 AM. Iftar (breaking fast) begins around 6:00–6:25 PM.

Daily Ramadan Routine

🌙

Suhoor (~3:30–5:30 AM)

The pre-dawn meal eaten before the Fajr prayer. Muslims eat nutritious food to sustain energy throughout the long fast. After Suhoor, the fast begins with the intention (Niyyah).

🕌

Fajr Prayer (~5:30–6:10 AM)

The first of five daily prayers. Marks the official start of the fast. Muslims recite Quran and supplications after Fajr.

☀️

Daytime (6 AM – 6 PM)

Complete abstinence from food, drink, smoking, and immoral behaviour. Muslims work, study, and go about daily activities while fasting. Extra Quran recitation encouraged.

🌅

Iftar (~6:00–6:25 PM)

Breaking the fast at sunset with the Maghrib prayer. Traditionally begins with dates and water, following the Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ, then a full meal with family and community.

🌟

Tarawih Prayers (8–10 PM)

Special nightly prayers performed only during Ramadan, usually in the mosque in congregation. Muslims pray 8–20 extra Rak'ahs reciting the Quran, aiming to complete the entire Quran by the end of Ramadan.

✨

Laylat al-Qadr (Last 10 nights)

The Night of Power — the most sacred night of the Islamic year, falling on one of the odd nights of the last 10 days. In 2026, it is expected on the night of 16 March (27th Ramadan).

🤲
Zakat & Charity

Ramadan multiplies rewards for good deeds. Muslims increase charitable giving (Sadaqah) and many pay Zakat al-Fitr — a compulsory charity before Eid al-Fitr to help the poor celebrate.

🕌
Mosques in Taiwan

Taipei Grand Mosque (清真寺), Taichung Mosque, Tainan Mosque, Longgang Mosque, and Kaohsiung Islamic Center all hold special Ramadan Tarawih prayers and Iftar gatherings.

🌙
The Three Ashras

Ramadan is divided into three 10-day periods: 1st Ashra (Mercy), 2nd Ashra (Forgiveness), 3rd Ashra (Salvation from Hellfire). Each carries special supplications and significance.

Suhoor, Iftar & Ramadan Food

Food plays a central spiritual and social role in Ramadan. The pre-dawn meal (Suhoor) fuels the fast, while Iftar — breaking the fast at sunset — is a joyful communal celebration. Here's what Muslims eat and how they break their fast during Ramadan.

🌙 Suhoor — The Pre-Dawn Meal

Eaten before Fajr (around 3:30–5:30 AM), Suhoor should be nutritious and filling to sustain energy through the day's fast. The Prophet ﷺ called Suhoor a blessed meal and encouraged Muslims not to skip it.

Common Suhoor foods: Oats, eggs, whole grain bread, yoghurt, dates, bananas, rice, lentils, and plenty of water to stay hydrated throughout the day.

💡 Tip

In Taiwan, many Muslim families eat congee (rice porridge), eggs, or noodles for Suhoor — blending their cultural food traditions with Islamic practice.

🌅 Iftar — Breaking the Fast

At sunset, the Maghrib prayer is called and Muslims break their fast — traditionally with an odd number of dates and water, following the Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ. Then they pray Maghrib before sitting down to the full Iftar meal.

Iftar is a joyous communal event. Families, friends, and mosque communities gather to share food, strengthen bonds, and celebrate the completion of another day's fast.

What Breaks the Fast?

Eating, drinking (including water), smoking, intentional vomiting, and intimate relations between spouses break the fast. Unintentional eating (forgetting) does not break the fast according to Islamic jurisprudence.

✅
Who is Exempt?

The sick, elderly, pregnant or breastfeeding women, travelers, and young children are exempt from fasting. Those who miss days must make them up later (Qada) or pay Fidya (feeding the poor) if unable to fast.

Prayers & Quran Recitation

Ramadan is above all a month of heightened worship. Muslims perform the five daily prayers, add the special Tarawih nightly prayers, recite the Quran, make extra supplications (Du'a), and seek the blessings of Laylat al-Qadr — the Night of Power.

📖 Quran Recitation in Ramadan

Ramadan is called "Shahr al-Quran" — the Month of the Quran. Muslims aim to complete reciting the entire Holy Quran (30 Juz / parts) over the 30 days, reading one Juz per day.

In mosques, the Imam leads Tarawih prayers reciting the Quran from memory. Many Muslims follow along with their own copies or apps. Quran recitation circles (Halaqas) are held daily in mosques across Taiwan during Ramadan.

🌟 Laylat al-Qadr 2026

The Night of Power is expected on 16 March 2026 (27th Ramadan). It is better than 1,000 months. Muslims spend this night in prayer, Quran recitation, and supplication.

📿
Dhikr & Tasbih

Muslims repeat phrases like SubhanAllah (Glory to God), Alhamdulillah (Praise God), and Allahu Akbar (God is Greatest) throughout the day, especially after prayers. Prayer beads (Tasbih) help track the repetitions.

🤲
Du'a (Supplication)

Du'a is the personal conversation between a Muslim and Allah. During Ramadan — especially at Iftar time, during Tarawih, and on Laylat al-Qadr — Du'as are considered especially powerful and likely to be answered.

🕋
I'tikaf (Retreat)

During the last 10 days of Ramadan, some Muslims perform I'tikaf — a spiritual retreat inside the mosque, dedicating themselves entirely to worship, prayer, and seeking Laylat al-Qadr.
