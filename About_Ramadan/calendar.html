What is the Islamic (Hijri) Calendar?

The Islamic calendar — known as the Hijri calendar (التقويم الهجري) — is a purely lunar calendar consisting of 12 months totaling 354 or 355 days per year. Because it is approximately 10–11 days shorter than the Gregorian (solar) calendar, Islamic dates shift earlier each year, cycling through all seasons over approximately 33 years.

The calendar begins from the Hijrah (622 CE) — the year the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ migrated from Mecca to Madinah. Each month begins with the sighting of the new crescent moon, so dates may vary ±1–2 days depending on location and moon visibility. The year 2026 CE corresponds to 1447–1448 AH (Anno Hegirae).

🌙

Lunar Based

Each month starts with the sighting of the new crescent moon. Months alternate between 29 and 30 days.

📅

12 Months · 354 Days

The Hijri year has 12 months and ~354 days — about 10–11 days shorter than the Gregorian year.

🕌

Hijrah Origin (622 CE)

Year 1 AH = 622 CE. "AH" stands for Anno Hegirae (Latin) or "After Hijrah" — the Prophet's migration to Madinah.

🔄

Annual Shift

Islamic dates shift ~10 days earlier each Gregorian year. Ramadan cycles through all seasons every ~33 years.

🌍

Global Variation

Dates may vary 1–2 days between countries due to differences in moon sighting methodology (local vs. Saudi Arabia).

⭐

Sacred Months

Four months are sacred in Islam: Muharram, Rajab, Dhul Qa'dah, and Dhul Hijjah. Fighting is traditionally forbidden in these months.
